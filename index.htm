The dataset is that of US airline connections. There are 235 nodes and 1297 edges. The network is undirected. The nodes represent airports and the edges represent the flights established between airports. To reduce cluttering, only nodes with a degree higher than 40 have been labeled.

The visualization uses point marks (i.e. the nodes). The channels used are color and size. The size of each node is directly proportional to its degree. Nodes with a low betweeness centrality are colored with a lighter shade of orange whereas a darker shade indicates higher betweeness centrality.

One structure that can be clearly observed from the network is that most airports with a high number of connections are clustered on the East Coast of the country.

The graph has 6 Modularity Classes.

The average Degree Centrality of the graph is 11.03. There are very few nodes with a degree centrality greater than 20 and, as can be seen from the graph, most of these are located on the East coast. This means that most airports only have direct connections to a small number of other airports.

The average Betweeness Centrality of the graph is 154.21. In general, the nodes in this graph have a higher Between Centrality than Degree. This tells us that while a particular airport/node may have fewer direct connections, these connections are linking different groups of airports together, thus increasing the importance of that airport.

The average Eigenvector Centrality is 0.15. A relatively low average score shows that many airports/nodes are not connected to airports of equal importance.
